I start by using a for loop, having i represent the reading frame 0,1,and 2. Then, with another for loop and j, I look at the sequence, three nucleotides at a time. Then I start another for loop and begin to look for a stop codon, also three nucleotides at a time. If it's indeed bigger than or equal to the minimum ORF length, then I add the necessary values to the keys in the orfdict dictionary. I then write two separate functions for random_nucleotide and random_amino_acid, with the first taking a nucleotide as input and randomly changing it into another nucleotide and the second taking an amino acid as input and randomly changing it into another amino acid by mutation. Afterwards I write two other functions, mutate_nucleotide and mutate_amino acids. The mutate_nucleotide selects a random position in the sequence and applies the random_nucleotide mutation function to that position, repeating 20 times with a for loop. The mutate_amino_acid function translates the initial ORF sequence into the corresponding protein and then repeatedly selects random positions in the protein sequence and applies the random_amino_acid function. Both functions accumulate mutations and print out the corresponding proteins that arise as each mutation is applied.
